Vinaya Pitaka - Monastic Rules

Bhikkhu Vibhanga - Pācittiya 14: The Training Rule on Lodging Requisites.

  • Translated from the Pali by Bhikkhu Brahmali.   (More copyright information)

Origin story

First sub-story

1At one time the Buddha, the Master, was staying at Sāvatthī in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. At that time it was winter and some monks put furniture out in the open in order to warm themselves in the sun. But when the time for departure was announced, they left without putting it away, getting it put away, or informing anyone. And the furniture was rained on.

The monks of few desires … complained and criticised them: “How can these monks put furniture out in the open, and then leave without putting it away, getting it put away, or informing anyone? The furniture was rained on."

2After criticising those monks in many ways, they informed the Master. … “Is it true, monks, that you put furniture out in the open … And, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Final ruling

3“If a monk takes a bed, bench, mattress, or stool belonging to the Saṅgha, and he puts it out or gets it put out in the open, and he then leaves without putting it away, getting it put away, or informing anyone, he commits an offence entailing confession."

4In this way the Master laid down this training rule for the monks.

Second sub-story

5Soon afterwards, some monks who were staying out in the open brought the furnishings back even in the appropriate season. The Master saw this, and after speaking on the Dhamma, he addressed the monks: “Monks, during the eight months outside of the rainy season, I allow you to store furnishings under a roof cover or at the foot of a tree or wherever crows or ravens do not leave droppings."

Definitions

6A: whoever … Monk: … The monk who is ordained by a complete Saṅgha through a procedure of one motion and three announcements, which is unchallengeable and fit to stand; this sort of monk is meant in this case.

7Belonging to the Saṅgha: given to the Saṅgha, given up to the Saṅgha.

8Bed: there are four kinds of bed: one with legs and frame called masāraka, one with legs and frame called bundikābaddha, one with crooked legs, one with detachable legs.

9Bench: there are four kinds of benches: one with legs and frame called masāraka, one with legs and frame called bundikābaddha, one with crooked legs, one with detachable legs.

10Mattress: there are fiver kinds of mattresses: a mattress stuffed with wool, a mattress stuffed with cloth, a mattress stuffed with bark, a mattress stuffed with grass, a mattress stuffed with leaves.

11Stool: one made of bark, one made of usīra grass, one made of muñja grass, one made of reed; it is stuffed and then bound together.

12Puts: puts oneself.

13Gets put: gets another to put. If he gets one who is not fully ordained to put it out, it is the responsibility of the monk. If he gets one who is fully ordained to put it out, it is the responsibility of the one who puts it outside.

14Leaves without putting it away: he does not put it away himself.

15Getting it put away: he does not get another to put it away.

16Or informing anyone: if he does not inform a monk, a novice monk, or a monastery worker, then when he goes beyond the distance of the stone-throw of an average man, he commits an offence entailing confession.

Permutations

17If it belongs to the Saṅgha, and he perceives it as belonging to the Saṅgha, and he puts it out or gets it put out in the open, and he then leaves without putting it away or getting it put away or informing anyone, he commits an offence entailing confession. If it belongs to the Saṅgha, but he has doubts about it … If it belongs to the Saṅgha, but he perceives it as belonging to an individual, and he puts it out or gets it put out in the open, and he then leaves without putting it away or getting it put away or informing anyone, he commits an offence entailing confession.

18If it is a ground-cover, a blanket, a carpet, a straw mat, a leather mat, a foot-wiping cloth, or a bench made of wood, and he puts it out or gets it put out in the open, and he then leaves without putting it away or getting it put away or informing anyone, he commits an offence of bad conduct.

If it belongs to an individual, but he perceives it as belonging to the Saṅgha, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If it belongs to an individual, but has doubt about it, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If it belongs to an individual, and he perceives it as belonging to an individual, but that individual is not himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If it belongs to himself, there is no offence.

Non-offences

19There is no offence: if he leaves after putting it away; if he leaves after getting it put away; if he leaves after informing someone; if he leaves while he is sunning it; if anyone obstructs it; if there are dangers; if he is insane; if he is the first offender.

20The fourth rule, the training rule on lodging requisites, is finished.

19p_2V_398, msdiv113Anāpatti— uddharitvā gacchati, uddharāpetvā gacchati, āpucchaṁ gacchati, otāpento gacchati, kenaci palibuddhaṁ hoti, āpadāsu, ummattakassa, ādikammikassāti.
