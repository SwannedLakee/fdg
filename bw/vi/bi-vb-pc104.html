Vinaya Piṭaka - Monastic Rules

Bhikkhuni Vibhanga

Pācittiya 104:Bhikkhu's Pc 8 Not to Announce to a Layman a Realisation That has Been Achieved.

  • Translated from the Pali by Brahmali Bhikkhu.

Origin story

1At one time the Buddha, the Master, was staying at Vesālī in the hall with the peaked roof in the Great Wood. At that time a number of monks who were friends had entered the rains on the banks of the river Vaggumudā. Just then Vajjī was short of food and stricken by hunger, with crops blighted and turned to straw, and it was not easy to keep oneself going by collecting alms.

The monks considered the difficult circumstances, and they thought: “How can we remain united and in harmony, have a comfortable rains, and get almsfood without problems?"

Some said: “We could do work for the householders, and they will give something in return. In this way we will be united and in harmony, and we will spend the rains in comfort and have no problems getting almsfood."

Some said: “There is no need to do work for the householders. Let us instead take messages for them, and they will give something in return. In this way we will be united and in harmony, and we will spend the rains in comfort and have no problems getting almsfood."

Some said: “There is no need to do work for the householders, nor to take messages for them. Let us instead praise one another’s jhāna, such a monk the second jhāna, such a monk the third, such a monk the fourth; such a monk is a stream-enterer, such a monk a once-returner, such a non-returner, such an arahant; such a monk has the three true knowledges, and such the six direct knowledges.’ And then they will give to us. In this way we will be united and in harmony, and we will spend the rains in comfort and have no problems getting almsfood. Indeed, this is the better way, that we praise one another’s super-human achievements to the householders."

2Then those monks praised one another’s super-human achievements to the householders: “Such a monk has the first jhāna … such a monk has the six direct knowledges." And those people thought: “We are fortunate that such monks have come to us for the rains. Such monks as these, who are virtuous and of good character, have never before entered the rains with us." And they gave such food and drink to those monks that they did not even eat and drink themselves, or give to their parents, to their wives and children, to their slaves, servants and workers, to their friends and companions, or to their relatives. In this way those monks were handsome, with rounded features, bright faces, and clear complexion.

3Now it was the custom for monks who had finished keeping the rains to go to see the Master. So when the rains was finished and the three months had elapsed, those monks put their lodging in order, took their bowls and robes, and departed for Vesālī. Walking by stages, they arrived at Vesālī and went to the hall with the peaked roof in the Great Wood. There they approached the Master, paid homage to him, and sat down to one side.

At that time the monks who had spent the rains in that region were thin, haggard and pale, their veins protruding all over their limbs. But the monks from the banks of the Vaggumudā were handsome, with rounded features, bright faces, and clear complexion. It is the custom for Buddhas, for Masters, to greet visiting monks, and so the Master said to them: “I hope you are keeping well, monks, I hope you are comfortable; I hope you spent the rains at ease, in concord and harmony, without dispute, and that you had no trouble getting almsfood?"

“We are keeping well, Master, we are comfortable; we spent the rains at ease, in concord and harmony, without dispute, and we had no trouble getting almsfood."

Tathāgatas sometimes ask knowing, and knowing sometimes do not ask; they ask knowing the right time to ask, and they ask knowing the right time when not to ask. Tathāgatas ask when it is beneficial, not when it is unbeneficial; in regard to what is unbeneficial, the Tathāgatas have destroyed the bridge. The Buddhas, the Masters, question the monks for two reasons, thinking: “We shall teach the Dhamma or lay down a training rule for the disciples."

4And the Master said to those monks: “In what way, monks, did you spent the rains at ease, without having any trouble getting almsfood?"

Then those monks informed the Master.

“But had you really achieved those super-human achievements?"

“Yes, Master."

The Buddha, the Master, criticised them: “Monks, how can you for the sake of your stomachs praise one another’s super-human achievements to householders? This will not give rise to confidence in those without it … And, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Final ruling

5“If a monk truthfully informs a person who is not fully ordained of a super-human achievement, he commits an offence entailing confession."

Definitions

6A: whoever … Monk: … The monk who is ordained by a complete Saṅgha through a procedure of one motion and three announcements, which is unchallengeable and fit to stand; this sort of monk is meant in this case.

7A person who is not fully ordained: anyone except a fully ordained monk and a fully ordained nun.

8A super-human achievement: jhāna, release, samādhi, attainment, knowledge and insight, development of the path, realisation of the fruits, abandoning the defilements, a mind without hindrances, delighting in solitude.

Permutations

Definitions

9Jhāna: the first jhāna, the second jhāna, the third jhāna, the fourth jhāna.

10Release: emptiness release, signless release, desireless release.

11Samādhi: emptiness samādhi, signless samādhi, desireless samādhi.

12Attainment: emptiness attainment, signless attainment, desireless attainment.

13Knowledge and insight: the three true knowledges.

14Development of the path: the four focuses of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of psychic power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, the noble eightfold path.

15Realisation of the fruits: realisation of the fruit of stream-entry, realisation of the fruit of once-returning, realisation of the fruit of non-returning, realisation of arahantship.

16Abandoning the defilements: the abandoning of sense desire, the abandoning of anger, the abandoning of confusion.

17A mind without hindrances: a mind without sense desire, a mind without anger, a mind without confusion.

18Delighting in solitude: because of the first jhāna there is delight in solitude, because of the second jhāna there is delight in solitude, because of the third jhāna there is delight in solitude, because of the fourth jhāna there is delight in solitude.

Exposition

First jhāna

19Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession.

20Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I am attaining the first jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession.

21Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I have attained the first jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession.

22Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I obtain the first jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession.

23Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I master the first jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession.

24Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I have realised the first jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession.

33Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the fruit of stream-entry … the fruit of once-returning … the fruit of non-returning … arahantship … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised arahantship," he commits an offence entailing confession.

34Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I have given up sense desire … I have given up anger … I have given up confusion, I have renounced it, I have let it go, I have abandoned it, I have relinquished it, I have forsaken it, I have thrown it aside," he commits an offence entailing confession.

35Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “My mind is free from the hindrance of sense desire … My mind is free from the hindrance of anger … My mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," he commits an offence entailing confession.

36Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna in an empty dwelling … the second jhāna … the third jhāna … the fourth jhāna … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the fourth jhāna in an empty dwelling," he commits an offence entailing confession.

Combinations of two attainments

37Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the second jhāna … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and the second jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession.

38Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the third jhāna … the first jhāna and the fourth jhāna … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and the fourth jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession.

39Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the emptiness release … and the signless release … and the desireless release … and the emptiness samādhi … and the signless samādhi … and the desireless samādhi … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and the desireless samādhi," he commits an offence entailing confession.

40Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the emptiness attainment … the signless attainment … the desireless attainment … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and the desireless attainment," he commits an offence entailing confession.

41Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the three true knowledges … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and the three true knowledges," he commits an offence entailing confession.

42Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the four focuses of mindfulness … the four right efforts … the four bases for spiritual power … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and the four bases for spiritual power," he commits an offence entailing confession.

43Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the five faculties … the five powers … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and the five powers," he commits an offence entailing confession.

44Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the seven factors of awakening … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and the seven factors of awakening," he commits an offence entailing confession.

45Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the noble eightfold path … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and the noble eightfold path," he commits an offence entailing confession.

46Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the fruit of stream-entry … the fruit of once-returning … the fruit of non-returning … arahantship … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and arahantship," he commits an offence entailing confession.

47Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and I have given up sense desire … and I have given up anger … and I have given up confusion … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and I have given up confusion," he commits an offence entailing confession.

48Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and my mind is free from the hindrance of sense desire … and my mind is free from the hindrance of anger … and my mind is free from the hindrance of confusion … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the first jhāna and my mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," he commits an offence entailing confession.

49Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the second jhāna and the third jhāna … the second jhāna and the fourth jhāna … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the second jhāna and the fourth jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession.

50Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the second jhāna and the emptiness release … and my mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," he commits an offence entailing confession.

51Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the second jhāna and the first jhāna … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … I have realised the second jhāna and the first jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession. …

52The basis in brief.

53Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “My mind is free from the hindrance of confusion and I attained the first jhāna … I am attaining … I have attained … I obtain … I master … My mind is free from the hindrance of confusion and I have realised the first jhāna," he commits an offence entailing confession. …

54Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “My mind is free from the hindrance of confusion and my mind is free from the hindrance of anger," he commits an offence entailing confession. …

Combination of all attainments

55Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the second jhāna and the third jhāna and the fourth jhāna and the emptiness release and the signless release and the desireless release and the emptiness samādhi and the signless samādhi and the desireless samādhi and the emptiness attainment and the signless attainment and the desireless attainment and the three true knowledges and the four focuses of mindfulness and the four right efforts and the four bases for spiritual power and the five faculties and the five powers and the seven factors of awakening and the noble eightfold path and the fruit of stream entry and the fruit of once-returning and the fruit of non-returning and arahantship … and I have given up sense desire, renounced it, let it go, abandoned it, relinquished it, forsaken it, thrown it aside; and I have given up anger, renounced it, let it go, abandoned it, relinquished it, forsaken it, thrown it aside; and I have given up confusion, renounced it, let it go, abandoned it, relinquished it, forsaken it, thrown it aside; and my mind is free from the hindrance of sense desire; and my mind is free from the hindrance of anger; and my mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," he commits an offence entailing confession.

Meaning to say one thing, but saying something else

56Informs: if a monk means to say to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna," while actually saying: “I attained the second jhāna," if the listener understands, he commits an offence entailing confession; if the listener does not understand, he commits an offence of bad conduct.

57Informs: if a monk means to say to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna," while actually saying: “I attained the third jhāna … the fourth jhāna … the emptiness release … the signless release … the desireless release … the emptiness samādhi … the signless samādhi … the desireless samādhi … the emptiness attainment … the signless attainment … the desireless attainment … the three true knowledges … the four focuses of mindfulness … the four right efforts … the four bases for spiritual power … the five faculties … the five powers … the seven factors of awakening … the noble eightfold path … the fruit of stream entry … the fruit of once-returning … the fruit of non-returning … arahantship … I have given up sense desire, renounced it, let it go, abandoned it, relinquished it, forsaken it, thrown it aside … I have given up anger, renounced it, let it go, abandoned it, relinquished it, forsaken it, thrown it aside … I have given up confusion, renounced it, let it go, abandoned it, relinquished it, forsaken it, thrown it aside … my mind is free from the hindrance of sense desire … my mind is free from the hindrance of anger … my mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," if the listener understands, he commits an offence entailing confession; if the listener does not understand, he commits an offence of bad conduct.

58Informs: if a monk means to say to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the second jhāna" … while actually saying: “My mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," if the listener understands, he commits an offence entailing confession; if the listener does not understand, he commits an offence of bad conduct. …

59Informs: if a monk means to say to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the second jhāna," while actually saying: “I attained the first jhāna," if the listener understands, he commits an offence entailing confession; if the listener does not understand, he commits an offence of bad conduct. …

60The basis in brief.

61Informs: if a monk means to say to a person who is not fully ordained: “My mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," while actually saying: “I attained the first jhāna," if the listener understands, he commits an offence entailing confession; if the listener does not understand, he commits an offence of bad conduct. …

62Informs: if a monk means to say to a person who is not fully ordained: “My mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," while actually saying: “My mind is free from the hindrance of anger," if the listener understands, he commits an offence entailing confession; if the listener does not understand, he commits an offence of bad conduct. …

63Informs: if a monk means to say to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the first jhāna and the second jhāna and the third jhāna and the fourth jhāna … and my mind is free from the hindrance of anger," while actually saying: “My mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," if the listener understands, he commits an offence entailing confession; if the listener does not understand, he commits an offence of bad conduct.

64Informs: if a monk means to say to a person who is not fully ordained: “I attained the second jhāna and the third jhāna and the fourth jhāna … and my mind is free from the hindrance of confusion," while actually saying: “I attained the first jhāna," if the listener understands, he commits an offence entailing confession; if the listener does not understand, he commits an offence of bad conduct. …

Gross hinting

65Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “The monk who stayed in your dwelling attained the first jhāna … is attaining … has attained … obtains … masters … has realised the first jhāna," has realised the first jhāna," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

66Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “The monk who stayed in your dwelling attained the second jhāna … the third jhāna … the fourth jhāna … is attaining … has attained … obtains … masters … has realised the fourth jhāna," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

67Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “The monk who stayed in your dwelling attained the emptiness release … the signless release … the desireless release … the emptiness samādhi … the signless samādhi … the desireless samādhi … is attaining … has attained … obtains … masters … has realised the desireless samādhi," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

68Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “The monk who stayed in your dwelling attained the emptiness attainment … the signless attainment … the desireless attainment … is attaining … has attained … obtains … masters … has realised the desireless attainment," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

69Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “The monk who stayed in your dwelling attained the the three true knowledges … the four focuses of mindfulness … the four right efforts … the four bases for spiritual power … the five faculties … the five powers … the seven factors of awakening … the noble eightfold path … the fruit of stream entry … the fruit of once-returning … the fruit of non-returning … arahantship … is attaining … has attained … obtains … masters … has realised … has given up sense desire … has given up anger … has given up confusion, renounced it, let it go, abandoned it, relinquished it, forsaken it, thrown it aside … has a mind free from the hindrance of sense desire … has a mind free from the hindrance of anger … has a mind free from the hindrance of confusion," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

70Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “The monk who stayed in your dwelling attained the the first jhāna in an empty dwelling … the second jhāna … the third jhāna … the fourth jhāna … is attaining … has attained … obtains … masters … has realised the fourth jhāna in an empty dwelling," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

71Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “The monk who made use of your robes … who made use of your almsfood … who made use of your lodging requisites … who made use of your medicines attained the fourth jhāna in an empty dwelling … is attaining … has attained … obtains … masters … has realised the fourth jhāna in an empty dwelling," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

72Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “The monk who has made use of your dwelling … who has made use of your robes … who has made use of your almsfood … who has made use of your lodging requisites … who has made use of your medicines attained the fourth jhāna in an empty dwelling … is attaining … has attained … obtains … masters … has realised the fourth jhāna in an empty dwelling," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

73Informs: if a monk says to a person who is not fully ordained: “The monk to whom you gave a dwelling … to whom you gave robes … to whom you gave almsfood … to whom you gave lodging requisites … to whom you gave medicines … he attained the fourth jhāna in an empty dwelling … is attaining … has attained … obtains … masters … has realised the fourth jhāna in an empty dwelling," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

Non-offences

74There is no offence: if he truthfully informs one who is fully ordained; if he is the first offender.

75The eighth rule, the training rule on informing truthfully, is finished.
