Vinaya Pitaka - Monastic Rules

Bhikkhu Vibhanga - Nissaggiya Pācittiya 29: The Training Rule on What is Dangerous.

  • Translated from the Pali by Bhikkhu Brahmali.   (More copyright information)

Origin story

1At one time the Buddha was staying at Sāvatthī in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. At that time monks who had completed the rains residence were staying in wilderness lodgings. Thieves who were active during the month of Kattika attacked the monks, thinking: “They have obtained things."

The monks informed the Master. The Master then spoke on the Dhamma and addressed the monks: “Monks, I allow monks who are staying in wilderness lodgings to store one of their three robes in an inhabited area."

2Knowing that the Master had made this allowance, monks stored one of their three robes in inhabited areas, and they stayed apart from them for more than six days. Those robes were lost, destroyed, burnt, and eaten by rats. As a consequence the monks became poorly dressed and wore shabby robes. Other monks asked them why they were so poorly dressed, and they informed them about what had happened. The monks of few desires complained and criticised them: “How can those monks store one of their three robes in an inhabited area and then stay apart from it for more than six days?"

3After criticising those monks in many ways, they informed the Master. … “Is it true, monks, that monks are storing one of their three robes in an inhabited area and then stay apart from it for more than six days?" “It’s true, Master." The Buddha, the Master, criticised them: “ … Monks, how can those foolish men store one of their three robes in an inhabited area and then stay apart from it for more than six days? This will not give rise to confidence in those without it … And, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Final ruling

4“After observing the Kattika full moon that ends the rainy season, a monk who is staying in wilderness lodgings considered dangerous and frightening may store one of his three robes in an inhabited area, if he so desires, so long as he has a reason for staying apart from that robe. He should stay apart from that robe for at most six days. If he stays apart from it beyond that, unless he has the permission of the monks, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession."

Definitions

5After observing: after completing the rainy season.

6The full moon that ends the rainy season: the fourth full moon of the rainy season in the month of Kattika is so called.

7Wilderness lodgings: it is called a wilderness lodging if it is at least one kilometre away from any inhabited area. The text reads ‘five hundred bow-lengths’, pañcadhanusatikaṁ. One bow length, according to Monier-Williams’ Sanskrit English Dictionary (SED), is equivalent to four hatthas (s.v. dhanu), often translated as ‘cubits’. (The hattha, again according to SED (s.v. hasta), is equivalent to 24 aṅgulis or finger-breadths.) Following Bhikkhu Ṭhanissaro’s suggestions in Buddhist Monastic Code 1, one cubit is approximately 50 centimetres, which means five hundred bow-lengths add up to about one kilometre.

8Dangerous: in a monastery, or in the vicinity of a monastery, thieves have been seen camped, eating, standing, seated, or lying down.

9Frightening: in a monastery, or in the vicinity of a monastery, thieves have been seen injuring, robbing, or beating people.

10A monk who is staying (in such lodgings): a monk who is staying in that kind of lodgings.

11If he so desires: if he so wishes.

12One of his three robes: the outer robe, the upper robe, or the lower robe.

13May store [ … ] in an inhabited area: may store it anywhere in his alms village.

14So long as he has a reason for staying apart from that robe: if there is a reason, if there is something to be done.

15He should stay apart from that robe for at most six days: he should stay apart from it for six days at the most.

16Unless he has the permission of the monks: except if the monks have given permission.

17If he stays apart from it beyond that: at dawn on the seventh day, the robe becomes subject to relinquishment.

The robe is to be relinquished to a Saṅgha, a group, or an individual. “And, monks, it is to be relinquished in this way. … To be expanded as in Nissaggiya Pācittiya 1, verses 13 to 17 with appropriate substitutions. … ‘Bhante, this robe which I have stayed apart from for more than six days without permission from the monks is to be relinquished. I relinquish it to the Saṅgha.’ … the Saṅgha should give … the Venerables should give … “I give this robe back to the Venerable."

Permutations

18If it is more than six days and he perceives it as more, and he stays apart from it, unless he has the permission of the monks, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession. If it is more than six days, but he has doubts about it, and he stays apart from it, unless he has the permission of the monks, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession. If it is more than six days, but he does not perceives it as more, and he stays apart from it, unless he has the permission of the monks, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession.

If the determination is not given up, but he perceives it as given up … If it is not given away, but he perceives it as given away … If it is not lost, but he perceives it as lost … If it is not destroyed, but he perceives it as destroyed … If it is not burnt, but he perceives it as burnt … If it is not stolen, but he perceives it as stolen, and he stays apart from it, unless he has the permission of the monks, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession.

19If he uses a robe which is to be relinquished without first relinquishing it, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If it is less than six days, but he perceives it as more, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If it is less than six days, but he has doubts about it, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If it is less than six days and he perceives it as less, there is no offence.

Non-offences

20There is no offence: if he stays apart from the robe for six days; if he stays apart from the robe for less than six days; if, after staying apart from it for six days, he stays within the village boundary and then departs; if within the six days he gives up the determination, it is given away, lost, destroyed, burnt, stolen, or taken on trust; if he has the permission of the monks; if he is insane; if he is the first offender.

21The ninth rule, the training rule on what is dangerous, is finished.

Netaṁ, bhikkhave, appasannānaṁ vā pasādāya … pe … evañca pana, bhikkhave, imaṁ sikkhāpadaṁ uddiseyyātha—

13p_1V_2412Antaraghare nikkhipeyyā'ti samantā gocaragāme nikkhipeyya.

17p_1V_2416Tato ce uttari vippavaseyyā'ti sattame aruṇuggamane nissaggiyaṁ hoti.

18p_1V_2417, msdiv655Atirekachāratte atirekasaññī vippavasati, aññatra bhikkhusammutiyā, nissaggiyaṁ pācittiyaṁ. Atirekachāratte vematiko vippavasati, aññatra bhikkhusammutiyā, nissaggiyaṁ pācittiyaṁ. Atirekachāratte ūnakasaññī vippavasati, aññatra bhikkhusammutiyā, nissaggiyaṁ pācittiyaṁ.

19p_1V_2418Nissaggiyaṁ cīvaraṁ anissajjitvā paribhuñjati, āpatti dukkaṭassa. Ūnakachāratte atirekasaññī, āpatti dukkaṭassa. Ūnakachāratte vematiko, āpatti dukkaṭassa. Ūnakachāratte ūnakasaññī, anāpatti.

 

20p_1V_2419, msdiv656Anāpatti— chārattaṁ vippavasati, ūnakachārattaṁ vippavasati, chārattaṁ vippavasitvā puna gāmasīmaṁ okkamitvā vasitvā pakkamati, antochārattaṁ paccuddharati, vissajjeti, nassati, vinassati, ḍayhati, acchinditvā gaṇhanti, vissāsaṁ gaṇhanti, bhikkhusammutiyā, ummattakassa, ādikammikassāti.
