Bhikkhu Vibhanga - Pācittiya 24: The Training Rule on Worldly Gain.

  • Translated from the Pali by Bhikkhu Brahmali.   (More copyright information)

Origin story

1At one time the Buddha, the Master, was staying at Sāvatthī in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. At that time the senior monks who instructed the nuns received robes, almsfood, lodging requisites, and medicines. And monks from the group of six said this about them: “The senior monks are not instructing the nuns to render a service, but for the sake of worldly gain."

2The monks of few desires … complained and criticised them: “How can those monks from the group of six say that the senior monks are not instructing the nuns to render a service, but for the sake of worldly gain?" … “Is it true, monks, that you say this?" “It’s true, Master." The Buddha, the Master, criticised them: “ … Foolish men, how can you say that the senior monks are not instructing the nuns to render a service, but for the sake of worldly gain? This will not give rise to confidence in those without it … And, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Final ruling

3“If a monk says that the senior monks are instructing the nuns for the sake of worldly gain, he commits an offence entailing confession."

Definitions

4A: whoever … Monk: … The monk who is ordained by a complete Saṅgha through a procedure of one motion and three announcements, which is unchallengeable and fit to stand; this sort of monk is meant in this case.

5For the sake of worldly gain: for the sake of robes, for the sake of almsfood, for the sake of lodging requisites, for the sake of medicines, for the sake of honour, for the sake of respect, for the sake of deference, for the sake of veneration, for the sake of worship.

6Says: if, concerning one who is fully ordained and who has been agreed upon by the Saṅgha as an instructor of nuns - desiring to disparage him, desiring to give him a bad reputation, desiring to humiliate him - he says: “He instructs for the sake of robes, for the sake of almsfood, for the sake of lodging requisites, for the sake of medicines, for the sake of honour, for the sake of respect, for the sake of deference, for the sake of veneration, for the sake of worship," he commits an offence entailing confession.

Permutations

7If it is a legitimate act of the Saṅgha, and he perceives it as legitimate, and he says such a thing, he commits an offence entailing confession. If it is a legitimate act of the Saṅgha, but he has doubts about it, and he says such a thing, he commits an offence entailing confession. If it is a legitimate act of the Saṅgha, but he perceives it as illegitimate, and he says such a thing, he commits an offence entailing confession.

8When someone who is fully ordained is an instructor of nuns, but he has not been agreed upon by the Saṅgha as such, and a monk, desiring to disparage him, desiring to give him a bad reputation, desiring to humiliate him, says: “He instructs for the sake of robes, for the sake of almsfood, for the sake of lodging requisites, for the sake of medicines, for the sake of honour, for the sake of respect, for the sake of deference, for the sake of veneration, for the sake of worship," he commits an offence of bad conduct. When someone who is not fully ordained is an instructor of nuns, whether or not he has been agreed upon by the Saṅgha as such, and a monk, desiring to disparage him, desiring to give him a bad reputation, desiring to humiliate him, says: “He instructs for the sake of robes, for the sake of almsfood, for the sake of lodging requisites, for the sake of medicines, for the sake of honour, for the sake of respect, for the sake of deference, for the sake of veneration, for the sake of worship," he commits an offence of bad conduct.

If it is an illegitimate act of the Saṅgha, but he perceives it as legitimate, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If it is an illegitimate act of the Saṅgha, but he has doubts about it, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If it is an illegitimate act of the Saṅgha, and he perceives it as illegitimate, he commits an offence of bad conduct.

Non-offences

9There is no offence: if he regularly gives the instruction for the sake of robes, for the sake of almsfood, for the sake of lodging requisites, for the sake of medicines, for the sake of honour, for the sake of respect, for the sake of deference, for the sake of veneration, for the sake of worship; if he is insane; if he is the first offender.

10The fourth rule, the training rule on worldly gain, is finished.

2p_2V_549Ye te bhikkhū appicchā … pe … te ujjhāyanti khiyyanti vipācenti: "Kathañhi nāma chabbaggiyā bhikkhū evaṁ vakkhanti— ‘Na bahukatā therā bhikkhū bhikkhuniyo ovadituṁ; āmisahetu therā bhikkhū bhikkhuniyo ovadantī’"ti … pe … "Saccaṁ kira tumhe, bhikkhave, evaṁ vadetha— ‘Na bahukatā therā bhikkhū bhikkhuniyo ovadituṁ; āmisahetu therā bhikkhū bhikkhuniyo ovadantī’"ti? "Saccaṁ, bhagavā"ti. Vigarahi buddho bhagavā … pe … kathañhi nāma tumhe, moghapurisā, evaṁ vakkhatha— ‘Na bahukatā therā bhikkhū bhikkhuniyo ovadituṁ; āmisahetu therā bhikkhū bhikkhuniyo ovadantī’ti. Netaṁ, moghapurisā, appasannānaṁ vā pasādāya … pe … evañca pana, bhikkhave, imaṁ sikkhāpadaṁ uddiseyyātha—
