Vinaya Pitaka - Monastic Rules

Bhikkhu Vibhanga - Nissaggiya Pācittiya 14: The Training Rule on Six Years.

  • Translated from the Pali by Bhikkhu Brahmali.   (More copyright information)

Origin story

First sub-story

1At one time the Buddha, the Master, was staying at Sāvatthī in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. At that time some monks were getting blankets made every year. They kept on begging and asking: “Give wool; we need wool." People grumbled and complained: “How can the Sakyan ascetics get blankets made every year, begging and asking, ‘Give wool; we need wool’? We only make blankets for ourselves every five or six years, even though our children defecate and urinate on them and they are eaten by rats. But these Sakyan ascetics get blankets made every year, begging and asking, ‘Give wool; we need wool’."

2Monks heard the complaints of those people, and the monks of few desires … complained and criticised those monks: “How can those monks act in this way?"

3After criticising those monks in many ways, they informed the Master. … “Is it true, monks, that some monks get blankets made every year, begging and asking, ‘Give wool; we need wool’?" “It’s true, Master." The Buddha, the Master, criticised them: “ … How can those foolish men act in this way? This will not give rise to confidence in those without it … And, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Preliminary ruling

4“If a monk gets a new blanket made, he should keep it for six years. Whether that blanket is discarded or not, if he gets another new blanket made in less than six years, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession."

5In this way the Master laid down this training rule for the monks.

Second sub-story

6At that time a certain monk in Kosambī was ill. His relatives sent him a message: “Come, bhante, we will attend on you." Other monks urged him to go, but he said: “The Master has laid down a training rule that a monk who gets a new blanket made must keep it for six years. Because I am sick, I am not able to take my blanket when I travel. And because I am not comfortable without it, I cannot go."

7They informed the Master. He then spoke on the Dhamma and addressed the monks: “Monks, I allow you to give a blanket-permission to a sick monk. And it should be given in this way. After approaching the Saṅgha, the sick monk should put his upper robe over one shoulder and pay homage at the feet of the senior monks. He should then squat on his haunches, put the palms of his hands together, and say: ‘Bhante, I am sick. I am not able to take my blanket when I travel. I ask the Saṅgha for a blanket-permission.’ And he should ask a second and a third time. An experienced and competent monk should then inform the Saṅgha:

8“‘Bhante, let the Saṅgha listen to me. This monk so-and-so is sick. He is not able to take his blanket when he travels. He is asking the Saṅgha for a blanket-permission. If it seems appropriate to the Saṅgha, the Saṅgha should give a blanket-permission to monk so-and-so. This is the motion.

9“‘Bhante, let the Saṅgha listen to me. This monk so-and-so is sick. He is not able to take his blanket when he travels. He is asking the Saṅgha for a blanket-permission. The Saṅgha gives a blanket-permission to monk so-and-so. Any monk who agrees to giving a blanket-permission to monk so-and-so should remain silent. Any monk who does not agree should say so.

10“‘The Saṅgha has given a blanket-permission to monk so-and-so. The Saṅgha approves and is therefore silent. I will remember it thus.’

11“And so, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Final ruling

12“If a monk gets a new blanket made, he should keep it for six years. Whether that blanket is discarded or not, if he gets another new blanket made in less than six years, unless he has the permission of the monks, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession."

Definitions

13New: newly made.

14Blanket: it is made by spreading out, not by weaving.

15Gets made: makes or gets made.

16He should keep it for six years: he should keep it for a minimum of six years.

17In less than six years: fewer than six years.

18That blanket is discarded: it has been given to others.

19Not: it has not been given to anyone.

20Unless he has the permission of the monks: in the act of making another new blanket, or getting one made, except if he has the permission of the monks, he commits an offence of bad conduct. When he gets the blanket, it becomes subject to relinquishment.The blanket is to be relinquished to a Saṅgha, a group, or an individual.

“And, monks, it is to be relinquished in this way. … To be expanded as in Nissaggiya Pācittiya 1, verses 13 to 17 with appropriate substitutions. … ‘Bhante, this blanket which I got made after less than six years without the permission of the monks is to be relinquished. I relinquish it to the Saṅgha.’ … the Saṅgha should give … the Venerables should give … “I give this blanket back to the Venerable."

Permutations

21If he finishes what he began himself, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession. If others finish what he began himself, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession. If he finishes himself what was begun by others, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession. If others finish what was begun by others, he commits an offence entailing relinquishment and confession.

Non-offences

22There is no offence: if he makes one after six years; if he makes one after more than six years; if he makes one, or gets one made, for the sake of another; if he obtains what was made by another and then uses it; if he makes a canopy, a carpet, a screen-wall, a mattress, or a pillow; if he is insane; if he is the first offender.

23The fourth rule, the training rule on six years, is finished.

6p_1V_2140, msdiv559Tena kho pana samayena aññataro bhikkhu kosambiyaṁ gilāno hoti. Ñātakā tassa bhikkhuno santike dūtaṁ pāhesuṁ— "āgacchatu bhadanto mayaṁ upaṭṭhahissāmā"ti.
